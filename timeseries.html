Decomposition of a series

A time series of length \(n\) is represented by:

\[\{x_t~:~t~=~1,2,...,n\}~=~\{x_1,x_2,...x_n\}\] This consists of \(n\) values, sampled at discrete times \(1, 2, ..., n\). This will be denoted as \(\{x_t\}\) when the length \(n\) of the series does note need to be specified.

The time series model is a sequence of random variables and the observed time series is considered a realisation from the model.

We use the same notation for both and rely on the context to make the distinction.

An overline is used to denote the sample means:

\[\bar{x} = \sum{\frac{x_i}{n}}\]

The “hat” will be usde to denote the prediction or the forcast.

For example, with the series \(\{x_t~:~t=1,...n\}~, ~\hat{x}_{t+k|t}\) is the forecast made at time \(t\) for a future value at time \(t + k\).

A forecast is a predicted future value and the number of timesteps into the future is the lead time (\(k\))

Following the convention for timeseries notation \(\hat{x}_{t+k|t}\) can either be the numeric prediction or the random variable depending on the context.

Models

As the first two examples showed, many series are dominated by a trend and/or seasonal effects, so the models in this section are based on these components. A simple additive decomposition model is given by:

\[x_t = m_t + s_t + z_t\]

Where at a time \(t\):

  • \(x_t\) is the observed series
  • \(m_t\) is the trend
  • \(s_t\) is the seasonal effect
  • \(z_t\) is an error term that is in general, a sequence of correlated random variables with mean equal to zero

In theis section we will cover approaches to extracting the trend \(m_t\) and the seasonal effect \(s_t\) in R.

If the seasonal effect tends to increase as the trend increases, a multiplicative model may be more appropriate:

\[x_t = m_t . s_t + z_t\]

If the random variation is modelled by a multiplicative factor and the variable is positive, an aditive decomposition model for \(log(x_t)\) can be used:

\[log(x_t) = m_t + s_t + z_t\] If the random series \(z_t\) are normally distributed with a mean \(0\) and a variance \(\sigma^2\), then the predicted mean value at time \(t\) based on the equation above, is given by:

\[\hat{x_t} = e^{m_t + s_t}e^{0.5\sigma^2}\]

Estimating trends and seasonal effects

There are various ways to estimate the trend \(m_t\) at time \(t\), but a relatively simple procedure, which is available in R and does not assume any specific form is to calculate a moving average centered on \(x_t\).

A moving average is an average of a specified number of time series values around each value in the time series, with the exception of the first and last few terms. In this context, the length of the moving average is chosen to average out athe seasonal effects which can be estimated later.

To average out seasonal effects with integer number of moving averages, we need to center the moving average. This is done by averaging January up to December then averaging Feburary (\(t=2\)) up to January (\(t=13\)). This average og the two movign averages corresponds to \(t=7\).

The trend at time \(t\) can be estimated by the centered moving average:

\[\hat{m_t} = \frac{\frac{1}{2}x_{t-6} + x_{t-5}+ ... +x_{t-1} + x_{t}+ x_{t+1} + x_{t-+5}+\frac{1}{2}x_{t+6}}{12}\]

where \(t=7,...,n-6\)

The coefficients for each month in the equation are \(\frac{1}{12}\) or \(\frac{1}{2}\) in the case of the first and last terms of the series so that equal weight is given to each month and the coefficients sum to 1. By using the seasonal frequency for the coefficients in the moving average, the procedure generalises for any seasonal frequency (e.g., quarterly series), provided the condition that the coefficients sum to unity.

An estimate for the monthly additive effect(\(s_t\)) at time \(t\) can be obtained by subtracting \(\hat{m_t}\):

\[\hat{s_t} = x_t - \hat{m_t}\]

If the monthly effect is multiplicative, the estimate is given by division:

\[\hat{s_t} = \frac{x_t}{\hat{m_t}}\]

Expectation and the Ensemble

Expected Value

The expected value (Expectation (\(E\))) of a variable or of a function of of a variable, is its mean value in a population.

So \(E(x)\) is the mean of \(x\), denoted \(\mu\) and \(E[(x-\mu)^2]\) is the mean of the squared deviations about \(\mu\), better known as the variance \(\sigma^2\) of \(x^2\).

The standard deviation \(\sigma\) is the square root of the variance. Ifthere are two variables (x,y), the variance may be generalised to the covariance, \(\gamma(x,y)\).

Covariance

Covariance is defined by:

\[\gamma(x,y) = E[(x-\mu_x)(y-\mu_y)]\]

The covariance is a measure of linear association between two variables \((x, y)\). Importantly, a linear association between variables does not imply causality.

Sample estimates are obtained by adding the appropriate function of the individual data values and division by \(n\), or in the case of variance and covariance, \(n-1\), to give unbiased estimators.

For example, if we have \(n\) data pairs, \((x_i, y_i)\), the sample covariance is given by:

\[Cov(x, y) = \sum ~ \frac{(x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{(n-1)}\]

If these data points are plotted, the lines \(x=\bar{x}\) and \(y=\bar{y}\) divide the plot into quadrants. Points in the lower left quadrant have both \((x_i - \bar{x})\) and \((y_i - \bar{y})\) negative, so the product that contributes to the covariance is positive. Points in the upper right quadrant also make a positive contribution.

The covariance can be calculated in R by using te cov() function.

Correlation

Correlation is a dimensionless measure of the linear association between a pair of variables \((x,y)\) and is obtained by standardising the covariance by dividing it by the product of the standard deviations of the variables.

Correlation takes a value between -1 and 1, with a value of 0 indicating no linear association between the two.

The popular correlation \(\rho\), between a pair of variables, is defined by:

\[\rho(x, y) = \frac{E[(x-\mu_x)(y-\mu_y)]}{\sigma_x \sigma_y} = \frac{\gamma(x,y)}{\sigma_x \sigma_y}\]

The sample correlation, \(Cor\) is an estimate of \(\rho\) and is calculated as:

\[Cor(x,y) = \frac{Cov(x,y)}{sd(x)sd(y)}\]

# Calculate the correlation
cov(x, y) / (sd(x)*sd(y))
## [1] 0.3550973
# Use the Cor() function
cor(x, y)
## [1] 0.3550973

The ensemble and stationarity

The mean function of a time series model is:

\[\mu(t) = E(x_t)\]

This is, in general a function of \(t\). The expectation in this definition is an average taken across the ensemble of all the possible time series that might have been produced by the time series model.

The ensemble constitutes the entire population. If we have a time series model, we can simulate more than one time series.

However, with historical data, we usually only have a single time series so all we can do, without assuming a mathematical structure for the trend, is to estimate the mean at each sample point by the corresponding observed value.

In practice, we make estimates of any apparent trend and seasonal effects in our data and remove them, using decompose() for example, to obtain time series of the random component.

If the mean function is constant, we say that the time series model is stationary in the mean. The sample estimate of the population mean \(\mu\), is the sample mean \(\bar{x}\).

\[\bar{x} = \sum^{n}_{t=1}{\frac{x_t}{n}}\] The equation above does rely on an assumption that a sufficiently long time series characterises the hypothetical model. Such models are known as ergodic, and the models in this book are all ergodic.

Ergodic Series

A time series model that is stationary in the mean is ergodic in the mean if the time average for a single time series tends to the ensemble mean as the length of the time series increases:

\[\lim_{n \rightarrow \infty}~\frac{\sum{x_t}}{n} = \mu\]

Variance function

The variance function of a time series model that is stationaty in the mean, is:

\[\sigma^2 (t) = E[(x_t - \mu)^2]\]

Which can, in principle, take a different value at every time \(t\). But we cannot estimate variance at each time point from a single time series. To progress, we must make some simplfying assumption.

If we assume that the model is stationary in the variance, this constant population variance \(\sigma^2\), can be estimated from the sample variance:

\[Var(x) = \frac{\sum(x_t - \bar{x})^2}{n-1}\]

In a time series analysis, sequential observations may be correlated. If the correlation is positive, \(Var(x)\) will tend to underestimate the population variance in short series, as successive observations tend to be relatively similar.

In most cases, this does not present a problem, since the bias decreases rapidly as the length of \(n\) increases.

Autocorrelation

The mean and variance play an important role in the study of statistical distributions because they summarise two key distributional properties – a central location and the spread.

Similarly, in the study of time series models, a key role is played by the second-order properties, which include the mean, variance, and serial correlation.

Second order properties:

  • mean
  • variance
  • serial correlation

Consider a time series model that is stationary in the mean and the variance. The variables may be correlated, and the model is second-order stationary if the correlation between variables depends only on the number of time steps separating them.

  • The number of time steps between the variables is known as the lag.
  • A correlation of a variable with itself at different times is known as autocorrelation or serial correlation.

If a time series model is second-order stationary, we can define an autocovariance function (\(acvf\) (\(\gamma_k\))), as a function of the lag \(k\):

\[\gamma_k = E[(x_t - \mu)(x_{t+k} - \mu)]\]

The function \(\gamma_k\) does not depend on \(t\), because the expectation, which is across the ensemble, is the same at all times \(t\). This definition follows naturally from the equation for variance, by replacing \(x\) with \(x_t\) and \(x_{t+k}\) and noting that the mean \(\mu\) is the main of both\(x_t\) and \(x_{t+k}\). The lag autocorrelation function (\(acf\) (\(\rho_k\))) is defined by:

\[\rho_k = \frac{\gamma_k}{\sigma^2}\]

It follows from the definition that \(\rho_0\) is 1.

The \(acvf\) and \(acf\) can be estimated from a time series by their sample equivalents. The sample \(acvf\), \(c_k\), is calculated as:

\[c_k = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{t=1}^{n-k} (x_t - \bar{x})(x_{t+k} - \bar{x})\]

Note that the autocovariance at lag 0, \(c_0\), is the variance calculated with a denominator \(n\). Also, a denominator \(n\) is used when calculating \(c_k\), although only \(n-k\) terms are added to form the numerator.

The \(acf\) is defined as:

\[r_k = \frac{c_k}{c_0}\]

We’ll demonstrate the calculations in R using a time series of wave heights (mm relative to still water level) measured at the centre of a wave tank. The sampling interval is 0.1 second and the record length is 39.7 seconds. The waves were generated by a wave maker driven by a pseudo-random signal that was programmed to emulate a rough sea. There is no trend and no seasonal period, so it is reasonable to suppose the time series is a realisation of a stationary process.

The dotted lines on the correlogram are drawn at:

\[-\frac{1}{n} \pm \frac{2}{\sqrt{n}}\] where:

  • \(-\frac{1}{n}\) is the mean
  • \(\frac{1}{n}\) is the variance

If \(r_k\) falls outside of these lines, we have evidence against the null hypothesis that \(\rho_k=0\) at the \(5\%\) level.

We have to be careful when interpreting muptiple hypothesis tests. Firstly, if \(\rho_k\) does equal 0 at all lags \(k\), then we would expect \(5\%\) of the estimates \(r_k\) to fall out side of the lines. Also, as the \(r_k\) is correlated, we would expect the neighboring values to be statistically significant.

In the meantime, it is worth looking for statistically significant values at specific lags that have some practical meaning (for example, the lag that corresponds to the seasonal period, when there is one). For monthly series, a significant autocorrelation at lag 12 might indicate that the seasonal adjustment is not adequate.

The Correlogram

The main use of the correlogram is to detect autocorrelations in the time series after we have removed an estimate of the trend and seasonal variation.

In the code below, the air passenger series is seasonally adjusted and the trend removed using decompose(). To plot the random component and draw the correlogram, we need to remember that one of the consequences of using a centered moving average of 12 months to smooth the time series (and thereby estimate the trend), is that the first and last six terms cannot be calculated.

# Load data
AP <- AirPassengers
# Decompose using a multiplicative model
AP.decom <- decompose(AP, "multiplicative")
# Convert decomposed random component to timeseries
plot(ts(AP.decom$random[7:138]), col = "blue", ylab = "Air Passengers [7:138]", main = "Air Passengers Random Component [7:138]")

# ACF of random component
acf(AP.decom$random[7:138], col = "red", xlab = "Lag (Months)", main="ACF Random Component Air Passengers")

# Standard deviation of raw data
sd(AP[7:138])
## [1] 109.4187
# Standard deviation of random component
sd(AP.decom$random[7:138])
## [1] 0.0333884

The damped cosine shape of the acf() is characteristic of an autoregressive model of order 2 or suggests that the seasonal adjustment has not been entirely effective. The latter explanation seems unlikely however, as the decomposition does estimate twelve independant monthly indicies.

If we investigate further, we can see that the standard deviation of the original series from July until June is 109. The standard deviation of the series after subtracting the trend estimate is 41, and the standard deviation after the seasonal adjustment is just 0.03.

The reduction in the standard deviation shows that the seasonal adjustment has been very effective.

Forcasting Strategies

Purpose

A very efficient method of forecasting one variable is to find a related variable that leads it by one or more time intervals. The closer the relationship and the longer the lead time, the better this strategy becomes. The trick is to find a suitable lead variable.

A second approach, common in marketing, is to use information about the sales of similar products in the past. The influential Bass diffusion model is based on this principle.

A third strategy is to make extrapolations based on present trends continuing and to implement adaptive estimates of these trends.

Leading Variables and Associated Variables

App.ts <- ts(Build.dat$Approvals, start = c(1996, 1), freq = 4)
Act.ts <- ts(Build.dat$Activity, start = c(1996, 1), freq = 4)

ts.plot(App.ts, Act.ts, col = c(2, 4), main = "Building Application Activity and Approvals", ylab ="Dwellings per Month (AU$)", xlab ="Time (Quarters)")

# Cross Correlation
acf(ts.union(App.ts, Act.ts), col = c(2))

Here we can see that the building activity tends to lag one quarter behind the building approvals. The cross-correlations function can be used to quantify this relationship. The plot shown above, plotting cross-correlation function vs the lag is referred to as a cross-correlogram.

The bottom right and top left corners show the acf for each individual variable, while the bottom left and top right show the ccf for both variables for positive and negative lags. The time unit for lag in this case is one year.

If the variables are independant, we would expect that 5% of the sample correlations would lie outside the dashed lines. However, Several of the cross-correlations at negative lags do pass these lines, indicating that the approvals time series is leading the activity.

The ccf can be calculated for any two time series that overlap, however, if the series have similar trends and seasonal effects, these will dominate.

So lets remove the trend and seasonal components and try this again:

# Extract seasonal and trend components
App.ran <- decompose(App.ts)$random
Act.ran <- decompose(Act.ts)$random

# print the acf results
print(acf(ts.union(App.ran, Act.ran), na.action = na.pass ,col = "blue"))
